Determination of Energy Band Gap in a Semiconductor using a PN Junction Diode

Object

To determine the band gap (\(\Delta E\)) of a semiconductor diode (Ge/Si) by studying the variation of reverse saturation current (\(I_s\)) with temperature.



Apparatus Required

Power supply (DC – 3 volts fixed), Micro-ammeter (0–50 \(\mu A\)), Electrically heated oven, Thermometer, Semiconductor diode (OA 79 / Ge diode), Connecting wires, Beaker and mounting stand.



Formula Used

$$ \log I_s = \text{Constant} - 5.036\,\Delta E \left(\frac{10^3}{T}\right) $$
$$ \Delta E = \frac{\text{slope}}{5.036} \text{ eV} $$
























Figure 1



















Theory

A semiconductor has two principal energy bands: the valence band (occupied by electrons bound to atoms) and the conduction band (where electrons are free to conduct). The energy difference between these bands is called the energy band gap, \(\Delta E\). At higher temperatures, thermal excitation allows more charge carriers to cross the gap, increasing conductivity.

In a PN junction under reverse bias, the observed current is the reverse saturation current \(I_s\), which is dominated by minority carriers. This current depends strongly on temperature and on the band gap as: \[ I_s \propto \exp\!\left(-\frac{\Delta E}{2kT}\right). \] Taking logarithms produces a linear relation between \(\log I_s\) and \(1/T\), enabling experimental determination of \(\Delta E\) from the slope.





Derivation of Formula

Starting from the temperature dependence: \[ I_s \propto \exp\!\left(-\frac{\Delta E}{2kT}\right), \] where \(k\) is Boltzmann's constant. Taking base-10 logarithm: \[ \log_{10} I_s = \log_{10} I_0 - \frac{\Delta E}{2k \ln 10}\cdot\frac{1}{T}. \] Converting constants to convenient units yields the empirically used form: \[ \log I_s = \text{constant} - 5.036\,\Delta E \left(\frac{10^3}{T}\right). \] Therefore the slope \(m\) of a plot of \(\log I_s\) (y-axis) vs \(10^3/T\) (x-axis) equals \(5.036\,\Delta E\). Rearranging, \[ \Delta E = \frac{m}{5.036}\ \text{eV}. \]









Procedure

  1. Assemble the circuit in reverse bias as shown in Fig. 1: diode connected to a fixed 3 V supply and micro-ammeter in series.

  2. Place the diode carefully inside the oven groove and fix the thermometer close to the diode so it measures the diode temperature.

  3. Switch on the oven and allow the temperature to rise to about 65°C. The temperature may reach ≈70°C and stabilize; then switch off the oven.

  4. Allow the temperature to fall slowly. When the temperature decreases, measure the reverse saturation current \(I_s\) at regular intervals.

  5. Record the temperature in °C and the corresponding current \(I_s\) (in μA) for at least 6–8 readings across the temperature range.

  6. Convert temperatures to Kelvin: \(T(\text{K}) = T(°\text{C}) + 273.15\).

  7. Compute \(10^3/T\) for each temperature and \(\log_{10} I_s\) for each current.

  8. Plot \(\log I_s\) (y-axis) versus \(10^3/T\) (x-axis). Fit a straight line and obtain its slope \(m\).

  9. Calculate \(\Delta E = m/5.036\) (in eV) and compare with the standard value (Ge ≈ 0.7 eV).

Precautions

  • Maximum temperature should not exceed 80°C.
  • If using silicon diode, oven must reach ~125°C.
  • Diode must be properly placed in groove for uniform heating.
  • Avoid parallax errors in thermometer and ammeter readings.
